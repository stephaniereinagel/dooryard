# Task Standards — "Done Looks Like This"

Purpose: set the bar for the recurring work so nobody has to re-explain and nobody gets nitpicked after the fact. If reality differs from what's here, we update this doc — not our memory.

Each task has:
- **Why it matters** (one line)
- **Steps**
- **Done looks like**
- **Common mistakes**
- **Flag Stephanie if…**

---

## 1. Morning chicken check

**Why it matters:** Early catch of illness, predator damage, or escape = saves birds and eggs. One sick bird removed early prevents a flock-wide problem.

**Steps:**
1. Approach quietly, watch them move for ~30 seconds before entering.
2. Count visible birds. Expected count: **[CONFIRM current count, e.g. 24]**
3. Open coop, let them out (if not already).
4. Look at each bird you can see: eyes clear, no limping, no missing feathers in clumps, no bloody spots, no wheezing.
5. Check nest boxes for eggs (collect now or at end of shift — see Egg Handling).
6. Refill feeder to **[CONFIRM: ¾ full / full / specific line]**.
7. Refill waterer (see Waterer standard).
8. Scan coop interior: any obvious mess, dead bird, broken nest box, signs of rodents?

**Done looks like:**
- All birds accounted for or anomaly noted in log
- Food + water topped
- Eggs collected or noted
- Coop door status set per current predator plan **[CONFIRM]**

**Common mistakes:**
- Rushing — you catch illness by pausing and watching
- Overfilling feed (wastes; attracts rodents)
- Forgetting to close the pop door / leaving the run gate ajar

**Flag Stephanie if…**
- Count is off by even one
- Any bird is fluffed up, closed eyes, tail-down, or standing alone
- Blood anywhere
- You see signs of digging at the perimeter

## 2. Morning duck check

**Why it matters:** Ducks are messier and drake:duck ratio can cause injuries. Their water gets foul fast; dirty water → sick ducks + bad eggs.

**Steps:**
1. Count: expected **[CONFIRM, e.g. 16]**.
2. Visual scan — any limping, feather loss (especially on back of hens = drake harassment), blood?
3. Collect any eggs laid overnight (ducks often lay before dawn).
4. Refill feed per **[CONFIRM]**.
5. **Dump and refill water every shift** — ducks muck it within hours. Scrub weekly (Wed, see deep task).
6. Check housing for escapes or predator damage at ground level.

**Done looks like:**
- All ducks present
- Fresh water
- Eggs collected
- No obvious injuries untreated

**Common mistakes:**
- Topping off water instead of dumping (keeps bacteria; ducks need it clean)
- Missing eggs in the bedding (they bury them)
- Ignoring a hen with back-feather damage (drake over-mating — separate issue; flag it)

**Flag Stephanie if…**
- Count off
- A hen is visibly plucked on the back/neck
- Any bird isn't eating, is wobbly, or has crusty eyes/nostrils

## 3. Egg handling

**Why it matters:** Customers pay for clean, safe eggs. Bad handling here loses trust and risks illness.

**Steps:**
1. Collect into a basket/bucket — don't pocket them.
2. Light dirt: **dry-brush** off with a clean dry cloth or fine sandpaper. **Don't wash** unless heavily soiled.
3. If heavily soiled: wash in water **warmer than the egg** under running tap, dry immediately, and **mark for home use only** (washing removes the bloom).
4. Sort:
   - **Sellable**: clean, uncracked, uniform size-ish, no stains
   - **Home use**: dirty, cracked, oddly shaped, very small/very large
   - **Discard**: smells bad, leaking, clearly old
5. Place sellable eggs into cartons **pointy-end down**.
6. Write date + count on the egg log **[CONFIRM: paper log location]**.
7. Store in **[CONFIRM: mudroom fridge shelf / separate cooler]**. Chicken and duck eggs in **separate** labeled cartons.

**Done looks like:**
- Clean cartons sorted + dated
- Cracked/dirty set aside for house use
- Count logged

**Common mistakes:**
- Washing eggs with cold water (pulls bacteria in through shell)
- Mixing duck + chicken (customers will complain — duck eggs are significantly larger and priced differently)
- Forgetting to log count

**Flag Stephanie if…**
- Count is unusually low for 2+ shifts running
- You see blood spots, very thin shells, or double yolks (just a heads-up, not urgent)

## 4. Waterer cleaning

**Why it matters:** Dirty water = sick animals, fast. This is cheap insurance.

**Steps (daily — every shift):**
1. Dump old water fully (away from enclosure, not inside).
2. Rinse with clean water, agitate once.
3. Refill.

**Steps (weekly deep clean — Wednesdays for ducks, Fridays for chickens if time):**
1. Full dump.
2. Scrub with stiff brush + mild soap (**[CONFIRM: brand — e.g. unscented Dawn]**).
3. Rinse thoroughly (no soap residue).
4. Optional sanitize: **[CONFIRM if you want this — small splash of white vinegar rinse, or dilute bleach per CDC]**.
5. Refill with fresh water.

**Done looks like:**
- Water is visibly clear, no slime on inside walls
- Waterer stable and level (doesn't tip)

**Common mistakes:**
- Topping off instead of dumping
- Scrub with a brush that's also used on dirty floors (cross-contamination)
- Leaving soap residue (animals won't drink)

## 5. Garden walk-through (daily — *observation only*)

**Why it matters:** Catching a pest, a broken emitter, or a thirsty bed on day 1 prevents a disaster on day 5. **This walk is not where you decide what to DO in the garden** — that's MyGardenCoach's job (https://mygardencoach.netlify.app). This walk is purely to see what's happening.

**Steps (10 min):**
1. Walk every bed in order.
2. Check soil moisture by sticking a finger 2 inches into a few beds.
3. Look for: wilted plants, yellowing leaves, visible pests (aphids, caterpillars, squash bugs, cabbage loopers), chew damage (rabbits/deer/bird), weeds taking over.
4. Confirm irrigation is running if scheduled (listen, look for wet soil under drip).
5. Note harvest-ready items (relevant especially on Fridays for stand).

**Done looks like:**
- You can describe the state of each bed in one sentence in the log ("Bed 3 dry; Bed 5 aphids on kale; Bed 7 ready to harvest lettuce")
- Anything urgent is flagged to Stephanie; everything else goes in the log.

**Flag Stephanie if…**
- Any irrigation line is broken/leaking (urgent — affects everything)
- A pest is widespread (not just a few)
- A bed is wilted badly (may be a systemic issue)
- MyGardenCoach is asking you to do something that doesn't match reality (e.g. "harvest tomatoes" but there are no tomatoes yet)

## 6. Weeding a bed

**Why it matters:** Weeds now = seeds later = 10× weeds next year. But sloppy weeding damages roots of what we want.

**Steps:**
1. Water the bed lightly first if dry (weeds pull easier from damp soil).
2. Start at one end, work systematically.
3. **Pull from the base**, gently — don't decapitate; get the root.
4. For stubborn: use hand fork to lift, then pull.
5. Toss weeds in the **[CONFIRM: compost bin / burn pile / chicken run]**. (Some weeds with seed heads should NOT go to compost — **[CONFIRM: Stephanie's rule]**.)
6. If you disturb a desirable plant's roots, firm the soil back down.

**Done looks like:**
- Bed is visibly clean of weeds over 4+ inches tall
- No holes or disturbed roots around desirable plants
- Pile of pulled weeds disposed of (not left on the bed — they'll re-root)

**Common mistakes:**
- Ripping tops off and leaving roots (they regrow)
- Leaving pulled weeds on damp soil (they re-root)
- Getting overzealous around seedlings

**Flag Stephanie if…**
- You're not sure what's a weed vs. a volunteer seedling — stop, leave it, ask at wrap-up
- A bed has more weeds than crop (bigger conversation needed)

## 7. Farm stand setup + opening (Friday)

**Why it matters:** The stand is self-serve once opened — the setup *is* the whole customer experience. A well-set stand outsells a sloppy one 3:1. You won't be there to answer questions, so the stand has to answer them for you.

**Steps:**
1. Haul tote + tables + shade to stand. Setup order: table → cover → shade → products → signage → payment.
2. **Tables:** flat, level, covered with clean cloth.
3. **Products by sightline** — highest-margin/visual-pop items (baked goods, flowers) at eye level and front.
4. **Eggs:** in shade or cooler. Chicken and duck eggs labeled **separately**.
5. **Pricing:** clearly visible on every category. Because it's self-serve, no item can be unpriced.
6. **Payment:** cash box with change for $20, QR/Venmo code visible and tested, pen for check backup. Clear instructions: "self-serve — pay here" with arrows to the payment method.
7. **"How it works" mini-sign:** one small sign that tells the first-time customer the whole flow ("Take what you want → pay in the box or scan the QR → thanks!"). This is critical for self-serve.
8. **Road signage:** road-facing signs upright and readable from a moving car. Flag/banner up if that's the current setup.
9. **Open for business:** flip the "OPEN" sign.
10. **Facebook post:** photo of today's lineup + price list + any specials. Template: **[CONFIRM: link or script]**. Post to Stephanie's page and tag any local groups per her list.
11. **Final visibility check:** walk or drive past the road frontage. From a passing car at 40 mph, can you tell (a) that the stand is open, (b) roughly what's for sale? If no, fix signage before leaving.

**Done looks like:**
- A first-time customer can arrive, choose, pay, and leave without anyone present and without confusion.
- Everything visible and readable from the road.
- Products in shade; no bakes in direct sun; eggs cold.
- Facebook post is live with today's date and photo.

**Common mistakes:**
- Forgetting the "self-serve, here's how to pay" sign (customers assume no one's there = closed)
- Burying baked goods behind eggs (cookies are the impulse buy)
- No price on flowers (#1 customer hesitation)
- QR code not tested before you leave (scan it yourself with your phone before you go)
- Facebook post delayed to afternoon (post at open = traffic peak)

**Flag Stephanie if…**
- Any product supply is visibly low *at open* (she may want to pull one item to extend the week)
- Road signage blew over or is damaged
- Anything about the setup feels "off" — unsafe, messy, confusing — better to pause and ask than open a bad stand

## 8. Farm stand closedown (Stephanie's job — reference only)

**Not part of the assistant's scheduled shift.** The shift ends at 12:00 with the stand open; Stephanie closes in the afternoon/evening. Documented here in case he's ever asked to help close on a one-off basis.

**Steps:**
1. Count cash, subtract starting float, record take in **[CONFIRM: log / spreadsheet]**.
2. Unsold items: eggs → back to fridge; baked goods → labeled by date in **[CONFIRM]** (sell Sat or family/staff eat); flowers → refresh water, next-day sale or compost.
3. Pack tote back the same way it came out.
4. Break down tables, shade, signage. Store in **[CONFIRM]**.
5. Sweep stand area. Nothing attracting pests.
6. Note: weather, what sold first, what didn't move, any customer comments or requests (from any notes left in the cash box).

**Done looks like:**
- Stand area empty and clean
- Cash reconciled
- Sales noted

## 9. End-of-shift log entry

**Why it matters:** It's how Stephanie sees the week without being there. Write it like you're briefing someone who wasn't present.

**Steps:**
1. Use the `shift-log.md` template.
2. Check off what got done.
3. For each issue flagged: 1 sentence of what + 1 sentence of what you did or what's needed.
4. Note any supplies running low.
5. Record egg count.
6. List 1 thing you'd like to ask at Friday wrap-up (even if it's "nothing today").

**Done looks like:**
- Someone reading it in 2 minutes can answer: did animals get taken care of? any problems? what's next shift's priority?

**Common mistakes:**
- "All good" with no detail — unhelpful
- Saving it all for verbal (defeats the point of the system)
- Not noting supplies until they're already out
